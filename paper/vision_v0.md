## The Needs & how we answer them
* Existing neurotechnology definitions and taxonomies draw their boundaries in seemingly axiomatic but in reality arbitrary, and device-focused conventions. 
    * Arbitrariness: the three implicit dimensions, but these need to be questioned. 
    * Device-focused: makes it difficult to consider TIPSS
* But because definitions and taxonomies are used as frameworks for downstream works such as TIPSS, it is important to generate them deliberately. Otherwise they can create blind spots for governance. 

* This paper argues that a taxonomy built for governance needs a different organising principle: **what the technology can access, infer, or alter about a person** — grounded in what the brain actually does (its functions and outputs), not what devices happen to measure.

## What makes this publishable 
1. **A human-centred scope boundary** most taxonomies ask "does it measure neural activity?" We ask "does it create distinct risks to TIPSS?" This reframing is not just semantic - it changes what's in and out of scope, and therefore what governance frameworks can see. 

2. **Exposing the definition-governance mismatch** Current definitions implicitly stack three filters (neural specificity, activity/function, device form). It would be useful to explore 
* what each filter would exclude / include, and 
* what TIPSS implications each filter has.
